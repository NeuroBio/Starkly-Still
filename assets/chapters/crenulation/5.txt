Tom.  An unremarkable name for an unremarkable man.  Heavy-set and balding, the once-married and once-divorced bachelor was approaching his mid-forties.

Decades ago, ambition had taken him from his rural hometown to the nearest city.  His limited education and credentials held him back.  It bound him to low-skill jobs.  But he found his niche.  His thick frame and resting scowl made an otherwise shy, softspoken soul look intimidating.

From his 20s through his 30s, he worked as a bouncer.  Pretending to be some tough guy had been fun.  Sure, the yelling hurt his throat.  But he loved that women saw him as a bad boy they could tame.  He never had that kind of attention at the farm.  Those infatuations were the beginning and end of his marriage.

Now, he was too old for the club scene.  And good riddance.  The shrill, mechanical music kids listened to these days grated his nerves.  He transitioned to the next best choice for someone with his skills: security guard.

Malls.  Amusement parks.  Some ritzy apartment complex.  He even worked the zoo once.  All those posts had too much noise and too many people.  So, when the night guard position opened up at the local museum, he went for it.

He applied in person.  Spoke with the manager, Kyle.  Turned out they rooted for the same football teams and hated the same golf pros.  Easiest interview ever.

With a fresh uniform and a shiny plastic badge, Tom was ready for his low-key job.  One where he would spend more time examining the exhibits than protecting them.  One where he could walk dim hallways alone with his thoughts.

Well, technically, he was not entirely alone.

The museum’s resident restoration artist, Sam, was a night owl.  He claimed he did his best work from 10 pm to 2 am.  Whether that was true or not, he spent his nights in statue storage with classical music playing on low volume.  Of course it was classical.

Sam was the opposite of Tom.  He had the friendliest face in the world.  Under that, he was an intolerable bastard.  Earned his degree in something hard to pronounce from some pseudo-ivy league.  Acted like he belonged in the Smithsonian.  Talked about this job being a “stepping stone.”

That noise made zero impression under Tom’s poker face.  Sam’s sorry state only reinforced Tom’s core belief: education was not enough to achieve your dreams.  Not in the modern era anyway.  He was glad he never wasted his time attending college.

However, there was more to the tension between these men than academic differences.  Especially since that time Tom trapped himself in statue storage.

That night, Sam was on his “lunch” break when Tom reached the room.  Tom’s rounds required a sweep of every exhibit, hall, and closet.  Including this one.  He hesitated at the door.  He <i>hated</i> statue storage.

Life-sized, people-shaped things gave him the heebie-jeebies.  He never pinpointed what bothered him.  He just knew it had started with clothing store mannequins.

The first time he really noticed them, he was on a date with the soon-to-be wife.  She demanded an audience when she went clothes shopping.  One day, he found himself staring at a fake.  At the faceless visage of a white, plastic man in business casual.

His eyes fixed on the mannequin.  Daring it to move.  It stayed still, of course.

Fingers snapped in his face, and he glanced towards his girlfriend.  She twirled to show off her digs.  He pretended he liked the gaudy skirt on her waist.  Joked that he liked that waist even better without it.  She flipped her hair in disgust.  But he knew she loved the attention.

As she disappeared into the racks to hunt up her next acquisition, Tom’s gaze went back to the mannequin.  It was in the same position as before.  Cold sweat beaded in his hands.  That stillness was wrong.

If the place Tom worked now were a <i>wax</i> museum, he would have run out screaming.  But he could survive a couple of statues.  Right?  His hand stiffly gripped statue storage’s doorknob.

Just a quick sweep.  He would stay near Sam’s workstation.  There was no need to check each locker-sized slot in detail.  No need to look for things hiding behind the statues.  There was no space for that anyway.

He unlocked the door and forced himself inside.

Hanging left, his uniform jacket shushed against Sam’s desk.  His flashlight passed over the right side, illuminating one slot after another.  Each contained a rust-encrusted steel statue.  Straps held the figures in place so they would not fall over.  Or walk out.

Sam’s job was to derust them and restore any missing pieces.  To fill the pits.  Polish the surface.  Tom did not envy him that position.  Getting up close and personal with those things would have—

Startling, he bumped into Sam’s current project.  A civil war statue venerating some southern captain or lieutenant.  In this case, Tom preferred the rusted side to the restored one.

Easing around the dead, metal soldier, Tom lit up the far corner.  Nothing amiss in the creepy locked room.  Big surprise.  Regardless, he sighed with relief as he turned towards the door.  Just as it clicked shut.

“Ah, Hell no!”  He bolted to the exit.  Tried to open it.  The handle was stuck.  He jiggled it up and down, trying to loosen it.  No dice.  He pounded on the door.  “Sam?!  Sam!  You back yet!?”

No response.

Swearing under his breath, he glanced back at the statues.  “SAM!  Come on!” He went for the handle again.  It shot down on its own.  Sam opened the door.

Tom exhaled heavily.  “Sam, man, you don’t—”

“Are you illiterate?”

“N-no?”

Sam pointed aggressively at the sign on the door.  The one warning personnel to never be in statue storage with the door closed.  “What does this say?”

“I had the door open.  It shut on me.”

A disapproving sniff.  “Allow me to educate you.  This”—He bent down to pick up a metal triangle with rubber feet—"is a door stop.  It’s used like so.”  He placed it under the door, giving it a stubby kick to plant it tight.

“I thought it would stay open for the 30 seconds it takes to check the room.”

Knocking on the door, “Hear that?  That’s metal.  The door is heavy.  It’s tilted on its hinges, so it’ll close and lock when no one’s minding it.  It’s designed that way.  Think about it like a bank vault.  I’m sure you’re familiar with <i>those</i>.”

“Ain’t worked a bank.”

Sam’s smug smile deepened.  Darkened.

“And I ain’t never stolen anything either.” Tom shook his head.  Sam was not worth his breath.  “I got a round to finish.”  He shoved past Sam.

“It’s for your safety, you know.  Rust oxidizes.  That takes oxygen.  The stuff you breathe?  If you’re in there too long, you’ll suffocate.  Neither of us wants your corpse filthing that room.”

Tom gave Sam the finger and moved on with his night.

That had been three weeks ago.  They still exchanged glares every time they met.  They only spoke when Kyle looked over their shoulders, forcing them to play nice.

Tonight, Sam was mercifully absent when Tom reached statue storage.  Opening the door, he slid the stopper in place.  Gave it a mocking kick in salute of Sam’s arrogance.  Once inside though, the grudge was forgotten.  This room was far more terrifying without Sam in it.

The flashlight panned over the holding slots.  As usual, everything was in order.  Nodding, Tom turned to leave.  He reached for the doorstop.

Something scratched across the floor.  Fleeting, quick taps.  Tom’s shoulders sank with a full-body groan.  “Don’t tell me we got rats…”

His flashlight beamed across the floor.  Tile and more tile.  He considered letting it go.  No, he took too much pride in his work for that.  “Hate this place…”  He ambled back into the room.

Dropping to all fours, he searched under Sam’s desk.  Nada.  Nothing between the statues’ legs either.  The sound had come from the floor, right?  Or could rats climb?  He glanced up.  The flashlight followed his gaze along the upper shelves.  Another pass over the statues, in case the vermin had perched on one’s shoulder like a parrot.  Zilch.

As the flashlight beam fell, something caught Tom’s attention.  A glittering.  A gleaming.  The light refocused on a metal face.  Another heavily rusted soldier.  “The Hell…?”  Its eyes sparkled.  

He shook his head.  No.  Just no.  It was late, and he was seeing things.  He blinked hard to clear them.  Looking at the statue again, he still saw it.  That trembling sheen.  It transfixed him.  Anchored him.

He realized he had been wrong about the clothing shop mannequin.  It would have been ten times worse if it could move.

A click jarred Tom back to reality.  His head wrenched towards the closed door.  “But I put the—!  How!?”  His breathing tightened into a shallow pant.
	
The flashlight drifted back to the statue.  An accusing finger raised at the soldier.  “You…”  His hand dropped to his side.  Why bother scolding the effigy?  Even the genuine flesh and blood it reflected would have ignored him.

Pulling the desk chair out, Tom sat down.  “Sam’ll be back.  He’ll get all his nonsense out.  And then I’ll go home and have myself a beer.  Or three.” Though nodding, his eyebrows knitted together.

He glanced at the statue on the desk behind him.  A cherub boy releasing a dove.  Despite its meaning, the angel child was as unsettling as the soldier.  Except it had no rust.

Tom shifted to look straight at the cherub.  Impressive.  A few days ago, it was solid orange.  Sam had moaned about how hard it would be to restore it.  A load of bellyaching since it only took him a few days.

Tom turned back to the soldier.  Leveled his light on it.  Shot to his feet, dropping the light.  “What in the—!?”  Reclaiming the flashlight, he beamed it on the soldier again.  On its shiny features.  On its sleek pitted surface.  The rust was gone.  “Oh no, no, no.”

Rushing to the door, Tom stepped in a pile of crumbling something, slipping.  Crashing.  Rising to his knees.  His hands grappling for the door.  Yelping.  The handle was coated in sharp, crunchy dust.

He lit up his hand.  Burnt orange and caramel brown powder coated his palm.  Furiously, he wiped the rust onto his slacks.  Then he saw it.  The dust pile he had slipped on.  More rust.  Two rubber feet were buried in it.

“SAM!?”  He pounded on the door.  “FOR THE LOVE OF GOD, SAM, IF YOU’RE OUT THERE, HELP ME!  THE DAMN STOPPER DIDN’T WORK!  SAM!!”

A hissing wheeze silenced Tom.  Something behind him inhaled and exhaled.  Calmly.  Deeply.  He turned the light on the sound.  The room’s back wall was gone.  

In its place, slithered a wet, rusty goo.  Umber colors swirled and bubbled over its surface.  Sluglike fragments left and rejoined its base.  The only unifying movement was the swell as it inhaled.  The settling as it exhaled.

Sam did not make it back to the room that night.  A personal emergency called him home early.  The next morning, he found Tom.  He called for an ambulance but told dispatch there was no rush.  Tom’s body was clammy.

A month later, the door sported three new warning signs.  Sam jabbed a finger at them in disgust.  “These aren’t helping!  What we need is better training for security!”

Kyle shrugged helplessly.  “I already told Pat to use the doorstop.”

“Apparently, you need to show him how!  This is the second time that idiot has trapped himself in there.  He swears he’s using it, but it’s nowhere to be found when I get him out.  Honestly, I think he’s hiding them to spite me.”

“And why would he do that?”

“Hell if I know!”  Sam leaned back against the door, shaking his head.  “I almost wish we had Tom back.  At least he was quiet.  Pat’s a damn chatterbox.”

“Look, I’ll talk to him.  Again.”

“Can’t you just fire him?  It’s for his own good.  And mine.  I’ve met my quota for handling cadavers this year.”

Kyle put a hand on Sam’s shoulder.  “Please be patient.  For me?  I’m sure this’ll work out if we give him a little more time.”

Sam shrugged off Kyle’s hand.  “Fine.  Whatever.  He’ll be gone in a month anyway.  Just like all the others.”  He rolled his eyes.  “Security guards are all flakes.”  A huff.  “I’m taking a coffee break.”  He stalked down the hall.

Kyle slumped against the wall.  He gave the door a disparaging look.  “You need to be patient too.  I know you’re hungry, but if this happens again, the police are going to be suspicious.  Trust me, it’s a losing game once they get a sniff of something funny.  You have to wait for the nights we plan for.” He patted the door.  “It won’t be much longer.”
