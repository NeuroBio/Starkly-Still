Starting over had not been my choice.

Without an advisor to oversee my work—or rather, to fund it—my PhD thesis ended.  No more culturing rat neurons.  No more filling them with glowing proteins.  No more videos of cells growing processes.  Of their desperate desire to connect.

In truth, more often than not, my films were funereal.  The cast: overstimulated neurons from sick cultures.  They shriveled into apoptotic beads under the microscope laser’s light and heat.  In death, they formed a fireworks display of neon red, green, and yellow.  Beautiful in hindsight.  Crushing on shooting day, when every movie I had recorded since 5 am was trash.

I was on my third aborted project when it ended.  Third.  Was it really that many?

During my rotation, I had shadowed an older student.  Learned his topic.  Ran his experiments.  Picked up the lab’s techniques.

Meanwhile, I dug through the literature, searching for my own thesis.  I asked for guidance, and my advisor told me to read more.  I took that to mean I could research anything in our field.  That was so freeing.  Hypothesizing—imagining—what possibilities would answer our world’s yet-unknowns.  Formalizing my research plan in writing brought it one step closer to reality.

My proposal was shot down without hesitation.

Apparently, studying proteins related to traumatic brain injury was a <i>crowded space</i>.  It was the wrong direction to go.  Sticking to the lab’s expertise was <i>safer</i>.  I suggested other problems I had read about.  Every idea was disregarded.

I was told to resume a project my predecessor never managed to get working.  That was what my advisor wanted all along.  Guess what?  I proved it would never work.  It took two years to demonstrate that.

Next, I was forcefully reassigned to another project a student had struggled with and abandoned.  Guess what?  After a year, it was going poorly.

I suppose saying I <i>had</i> to start over is a lie.  When the lab closed, I could have fought for my neurons.  Someone would have kept my project limping along.  I never would have graduated, but it would have looked good on their tenure application.  Instead, I kept my mouth shut.

What I’m getting at is this: the opportunity to start over fresh in my 5th year was welcome.

In the new lab, my day-to-day centered on coding.  It was steady.  It was fast.  At the risk of jinxing it, I would even say it was fun.  It was a far cry from neuroscience, but if it landed my PhD quickly, who cared?

If I ever decided to return to studying brains, I had a solid excuse for why my thesis was in the wrong discipline.  Truly though?  I had already settled on never going back.  The scientist I was supposed to be had expired with the neuron fireworks.  That dream was over.

At least it was over for <i>me</i>.

It was lunchtime.  Three months after the old lab closed.  I sat at a table in the science building’s café.  I forget what I was eating.  All that matters is someone sat across from me.  As an introvert, I make my space cold.  Unapproachable.  Surely, whoever had joined me was someone who knew me well enough to look past that.  Before I could glance up, the words began.

“I’ve been looking everywhere for you!  How have you been?”  That unmistakable high-pitched voice began and ended with a rumble.  It carried a permanent, unstable, hazy note.  A tone that had once sentenced me:  <i>You’ll never make it in this world</i>.

My mouth hung open as I gawked at my old advisor.  She still wore that incongruous wig.  Its short, straight, red-tinged hair nothing like her natural, dark, frizzy curls.  A symbol of unnoticing.  The first effigy we all pretended was not there.

Just like we pretended that her mother was not hiding in her office to care for her.  For her sickness.  That her skin was not tinged Simpson’s yellow with jaundice.  That the deep bruising under her eyes was just a trick of the light.

To an outsider, she looked heavyset.  We knew how much had sloughed off her though.  She was a fraction of her original size.  As the excess mirth vanished, I wondered if she made jokes about it.  That she needed to lose a few pounds anyway.  How she looked better now.

I had no answer for her question.  She never cared about my words anyway.  For her, <i>how are you</i> was social grease.  If you responded with anything but generic pleasantries, the conversation ignored it.  Or she would admonish you.  <i>Stop being so negative</i>.

She barreled through the silence, “It’s been a while, but I’m getting everything up and running again.  I got my grants back.  I found the equipment other labs had… borrowed.  I got the whole misunderstanding straightened out.  I’m still working on regaining my DGS title though.  It’s hard when the interim DGS won’t give it up.”

And no wonder!  People quit because of my advisor.  Because of what <i>happened</i> with her.  It freaked them out that the university allowed something so egregious.  We had begged the department chair to help us—to help <i>her</i>.  In turn, she retaliated against us.

That conversation is burned into me.  Being closed into her office alone.  Feeling the weight of her deteriorating health.  Of her ever more frequent disappearances.  We knew something was wrong.  The entire department knew.  Her excuse?  <i>I’m just busy with my administrative duties</i>.

“But that will get settled eventually.  At least they can’t touch my tenure.”

I remembered when she was awarded tenure.  For the first time in weeks, she had shown up in person.  Just in time for lab meeting.  With ice cream.  She told us that we had to celebrate.  One lab mate had earned a first-author publication.  Three months prior.  Another had her first child.  Five months prior.  Events we had already celebrated.

But we ate anyway.  Spoons scraped bowls, entering mouths that only opened long enough to insert food.  What else could we do?

Finally, she dropped the bomb.  The real reason why she was there.  She reached full professorship.<i>
</i>
On a delay, hands raised.  Clapped slowly with uncertainty.  What else could we do?

The lab closed down a week later.

“Two new students are rotating right now,” she bragged.  “They look promising.  It’ll be difficult without other students to guide them though.  I still remember how to do everything, of course, but I won’t have time to teach them.  They need experienced hands at their side.  I’ve already talked with the rest of the old lab.  It looks like they’ll be coming back.  I want you too.  After all, it would be such a shame for you not to finish your project!”

<i>Such a shame.</i>  Her favorite phrase to lay guilt.  A veneer over her real motivation: her dedication to the system.  Students did work, so she could write grants, so she could fund herself, and so the university could take its cut.  She had to keep the conveyer belt—that meat grinder—running.

I knew my answer: No.  I swallowed hard.  <i>No!</i>  <i>Just say no!</i>

My voice failed me.  My silence was too well practiced.

“Think about it.  Really consider it.  It’s not like writing scripts is going to get you a good post-doc.  Let’s talk again later.  Maybe in a week?  It’s been great to see you!”

With that, she stood up and left.  It took her ten minutes to hobble to the elevator with her cane.

Why did they bring her back?  How could they let her coerce more students into her lab?  What inbred, cutthroat, academic circle jerk had she serviced to earn ultimate immunity?!

Because it <i>was</i> ultimate immunity.  She had died seven months ago.  Cancer.  It had eaten her alive for three years.  We learned what got her from the obituary.
