Mr. Bradford lived a small, solitary life.  Extravagance never spoke to him.  And his definition of “extravagant” was wider than most.

For example, he could afford a mortgage on a reasonable house with a yard.  However, he would never make such a purchase.  What business did a single man have in owning property?  It was a waste.  No.  His modest rental unit was sufficient.

A home’s purpose was to house one’s things and provide a safe place to sleep.  His 500-square-foot studio apartment did just that.  It held his mattress, a desk with a TV, and a kitchen.  The lack of closets made storage difficult, but he had cardboard bankers boxes for that.  He constructed a dresser out of them and thought himself clever for it.

The only stray items were his baseball, bat, and glove.  One day, he would pick up the game again.  Until then, his equipment sat in the far corner.  A reminder of his glory days from middle school.

There was one luxury he allowed himself.  His little dog, Sicilia.  However, Mr. Bradford contended she was <i>not</i> a luxury.  A luxury dog came with a pedigree.  Or was bought from a breeder.  Or at least came from a pet store, never mind the puppy mill that spawned it.  Sicilia’s origins were far humbler.

He had pulled her from a filthy paper bag lying in a dumpster.

In his vainer moments, Mr. Bradford claimed his four-pound companion was a chihuahua x Jack Russell Terrier mix.  Truthfully, she was an ambiguous mutt with a triangular head and bug eyes.  An ugly dog, but loyal and well-trained.  She knew the basic tricks: sit and lay down.  She nervously clung to Mr. Bradford when he was home.  When he left or returned, she would be at the window, her entire body shaking as her tail wagged.

Mr. Bradford worked inconsistent shifts as a cashier at the local gas station convenience store.  Were he ever late coming home, Sicilia knew what to do.  Her business ended up on a boot tray covered in newspapers.  Always without spilling any urine on the floor.

The only complaint Mr. Bradford had about Sicilia was her complex relationship with the Pomeranian next door.  When the dogs met outside, they were respectful.  However, if the Pom yipped during the night, Sicilia would run to the apartment’s wall and yap back.

It was a vicious cycle.  The two argued their cases with passion and intensity.  A pair of philosophers debating the meaning of dog existence on this earth.

Usually, the Pom was silenced before midnight by a smack from its owner, Ms. Pendleton.  If not, Mr. Bradford would heft up Sicilia, clamp his hand over her muzzle, and hold it until the Pom stopped.  Sicilia was too dignified to resume the discussion after the Pom went quiet.

All in all, Mr. Bradford’s life was simple.  Predictable.  Without vice.

Except for his hopeless addiction to the cocaine.  A habit he picked up in high school.  In his freshman year, he had changed districts.  He failed the new school’s baseball team tryouts.  They were of higher caliber than his middle school team.

Without sports to keep him busy, Mr. Bradford turned to other ventures.  He fell into a circle of losers and wannabes made cool by their access to cigarettes and booze.  Their one claim to local fame.  The group was discreet about the fact that their central member was a coke dealer.

Highs and trips, good and bad, were things of the past.  Mr. Bradford’s continued use represented a regimented medical campaign.  His measured doses were only enough to keep the withdrawal at bay.  He used at set times, so the chemicals would not affect his employment.  While not always successful in this endeavor, he was functional at work most days.

Perhaps a rehabilitation clinic would have been more appropriate for treating his sickness.  However, Mr. Bradford rejected the idea.  Too many acquaintances had left clean only to be seduced back to the drug.  They cycled in and out of the system aimlessly.  A couple had overdosed immediately after release.

True, half a dozen had stayed sober.  But they had more at stake.  A partner.  Children.  Family.  Religion.  These were the things that kept a man straight.  Mr. Bradford had none of these.  The odds were not in his favor.  His status quo was the only option.

And it was the only thing he could think about as he stumbled home late from his job one evening.  A well-meaning customer had missed the “no smoking” warning sign.  He caught himself on fire while lighting a cigarette at the pump.  Mr. Bradford had used the fire extinguisher on him and called for an ambulance.

Waiting for help took time.  Explaining the situation to the police took more time.  The longest part was showing them that, yes, every pump had shiny, new warning stickers.  A mandate after the last time this happened.

On the way to his door, Mr. Bradford made himself visit the community mailbox.  One junk packet for him.  Two envelopes for Ms. Pendleton.  Ads and coupons printed on newspaper sheets.

He crammed Mrs. Pendleton’s mail into her slot.  The junk mail went into the trash.  As for the fast-food coupons?  Some were for pizza.  His favorite.  He kept them.

Excited yapping greeted him as he passed his apartment’s window.  Sicilia’s jubilations set off the Pom.  By the time Mr. Bradford was inside, the dogs were deep in existential debate.  He picked up the boot tray, poured the urine and feces out by the door, and dropped it back in place.  He would swap out the newspaper after he finished clipping coupons.

Throwing the ads onto his mattress, he wandered to the refrigerator and peered inside.  Cooking disinterested him, so he kept minimal reserves.  The fridge door was stocked with condiments in single-use packets.  Beside them stood three open jars of whole dill pickles.  His second favorite food.  The shelves and drawers were empty except for an unassuming pizza box.

He opened it, hoping for two slices.  Only one.  A small one.  At least there was something.  And it had pepperoni.  He moved the box to the counter.  Opening a cabinet, he retrieved a paper plate.  Transferred the pizza onto it.  Turned to the microwave, and—

“What the hell?”  Mr. Bradford’s face screwed together.  His fingers probed the heating instrument.  This was not his faithful microwave.  It was new.  Fancy.  Sleek.  <i>Out of place.</i>

He scanned the kitchen, discovering a folded-over notice.  Gingerly, he opened it.  It bore the complex’s header, an overly formal greeting, blah, blah, blah.  The meat was buried in excess prose.  Something about electrical problems with his old microwave.

Mr. Bradford eyed the new device warily.  He glanced down at his plate.  Cold pizza was for breakfast, not dinner.  He popped it into the microwave.

Muttering to himself, he examined each flat button.  None said “Cook Time.”  In desperation, he pressed 1.  The microwave jolted to life, its digital display reading 1-:-0-0 in blocky, green letters.  Hardly ideal, but he could work out the light box’s exact machinations later.

Opening the silverware drawer, he reached behind an empty utensil tray for his pill organizer.  Each slot held his daily cocaine allotment.  He enjoyed setting it up each Saturday.  Measuring his maintenance doses with his electronic balance, metal sampling scoop, and weighing paper.

When he was in a particularly good mood, he donned his goggles from high school chemistry.  It made him feel like a scientist.  His second dream job after pro baseball player.

Mr. Bradford poured Friday’s powder into a line on the pizza box.  Meanwhile, the dogs’ barking rose in pitch and frequency.  They must have hit a contentious detail.  Their exchange degenerated into insult and fury.

“SICI!  SHUT THE HELL UP!”

Both dogs silenced.

Taking a plastic straw from the counter, Mr. Bradford inhaled his medicine.  His shoulders dropped.  He pressed the pill organizer shut and stowed it in the utensil drawer.  His eyes guiltily glanced at Sicilia.  She sat on his mattress, her tail wagging rapidly.  He mumbled, “Sorry, Sici...  You’re a good girl.”

The microwave beeped, and Sicilia danced into the kitchen to stand behind Mr. Bradford.  As he retrieved his pizza, her eyes implored him to share.

His hand hung on the microwave door.  “There’s nothing for you today.  Eat your kibble.”

Her nails slowly clicked across the tile as she retreated.

“There’ll be more tomorrow…”

Quiet crunching from the dog bowl.

He slammed the door closed.  The display read F-O-O-D.  Mr. Bradford’s mouth kinked right with an unimpressed frown.  “Yeah, I know.  I got it.”  He showed the pizza to the microwave.  The display read as before.

Scoffing, he grabbed the pizza box and tossed it onto the pile with its siblings from earlier that week.  Once settled into his mattress, he flicked on the TV.  He navigated to his recorded shows: the 8 am cartoons.  An embarrassing pleasure he was more secretive about than his cocaine addiction.

As Tom and Jerry dashed around the screen, he savored his pizza.  The buzz of even normalcy washed over him.  Smoothing withdrawal’s edge.  Sicilia curled up next to him.  He gave her a pepperoni slice.  Soon after, he fell asleep.  At 3 am, he awoke long enough to pull Sicilia away from the wall and hold her muzzle until the Pom went quiet.

Mr. Bradford’s drug dealer was in jail.  No one had picked up his territory yet.  When that bag emptied, there would be nowhere safe to get more.

Pouring in the last day’s ration, he stared at the pill organizer.  The idea of using every other day or halving his maintenance dose crossed his mind.  He lacked the willpower for that.  It hurt too much.  His drug-starved brain would dissolve into racing thoughts.  A paranoia conflicting with his lethargy.  He had to find a new dealer this week.

The pill dispenser returned to its home in the utensil drawer.  The dregs of his main stash slid under the bed.  He dug his phone out from under his blanket and ordered three pizzas.  Veggie supreme.  Pepperoni and sausage.  Some monstrosity with everything on it.  Enough to make it until Wednesday.

Sicilia vibrated with excitement.

The microwave blinked F-O-O-D in lime yellow.

She gave him a neutral look and left to continue her deliveries.

The boxes were warm.  Mr. Bradford would not need to use the godforsaken microwave.  He took a piece of the everything and the veggie supreme.  The rest went into the fridge.  Returning to the mattress, he eyed the pizza box tower.  It was time to do something about it.  A chore for tomorrow.

His evening was uneventful.  He ate his second serving of Italian fruits and vegetables.  Sicilia got two olives.  He took his medicine.  She resumed decrying the Pom’s philosophy.  Ms. Pendleton ended the fun around 10 pm.  Mr. Bradford got a full night’s sleep.

Mr. Bradford stuck his tongue out at it as he ate cold pizza for breakfast.  He contemplated using the oven to heat his lunch.  After all, pizza was born in the oven.  Surely it could go back in.  That was the decision he was struggling with when he remembered the pizza box tower.

With a groan, he gathered the greasy cardboard and headed for the door.  Sicilia weaved between his legs and dashed ahead of him.  Her nose pointed at the doorknob.  “Sici, no.  I’m taking out the trash.”

“GO LAY DOWN.”

Sicilia’s tail tucked between her legs.  She skittered across the apartment.  Curling up on the mattress, she gave the biggest, buggy doe eyes a dog could manage.

“Good girl.  I’ll take you later.”

One-by-one, he jammed his boxes into the compactor.  Roughly latching it shut, he waited for the grinding and crushing to complete.  He opened the door again and looked inside.  The compactor’s bottom was covered with the overflow.  He closed the door again and left, sure that it would clear itself this time.

Mr. Bradford settled on naming his new friend <i>Denise</i>.  To him, that name meant mystery, power, and sex appeal.  Appropriate for his new drug dealer.

Every day, after heating his dinner, he left a pizza slice in the microwave.  Every morning, he discovered a new bag with several ounces of cocaine.  With this new arrangement, he was set for life.

True, there was a cost to buying the extra pizza.  But it was pennies compared to the value of his growing stockpile.  He considered switching careers.  Drug dealer was a more lucrative position than convenience store cashier.  Plus, he would choose his own hours.  Be his own boss.

However, Mr. Bradford was overqualified for the job.  Being stopped by the police on the streets was a regular occurrence.  Each time, he emptied his pockets and let them pat him down.

“I know.  I’ve got ‘the look,’” he would tell them.

Perhaps once he got established, Mr. Bradford could deal from his apartment.  That was safer.  But establishing his customer base remained a risky endeavor.  If he stumbled onto potential buyers, he would reconsider the opportunity.  For now, he maintained his current employment.
