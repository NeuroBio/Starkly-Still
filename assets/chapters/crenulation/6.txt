The sky-blue Toyota Corolla hatchback’s open trunk was stuffed to the brim.  Ten-gallon buckets containing nets, snorkeling gear, a dive flag, and mesh bags.  “Check.” A boiling pot.  Bleach.  A metal dental pick set.  “Check.”  A suitcase holding 9 days’ of clothes, two swimsuits, and rash shirts.  “Check.”  Bottled water.  A cooler with lunch meat, bread, and cheese.  Granola bars and a cookie tin that was unlikely to survive the road trip.  “Check and Check.”

Gemma closed the trunk.  Or tried to.  It bounced back open.  Some extra oomph on the second attempt made it stick.  She patted her baby’s rump with a right-sided smile.

She popped herself into the driver’s seat and the keys into the ignition.  For a moment, she reveled in the engine’s hum.  Then she started her favorite playlist.  A mix of pop, rap, jazz, and Spanish ballads.

Reversing out of her spot, she drove to the apartment complex’s edge.  She hesitated there.  Looking back at the brick building, she saw her unit’s window.  The one she afforded all by herself with her first full-time job.  She blew it a kiss.  “Catch you later.”

She gunned it with a laugh.  Her destination?  Florida’s Atlantic coast.

The uninitiated would have called Gemma a “shell collector.”  She referred to herself as a “treasure hunter.”  There was a big difference.

A collector went to Sanibel Island and walked white beaches for an hour.  They picked up scallops, sand dollars, and maybe a 3-inch lightning whelk – and the whelk was a stretch.  After that, they perused the shell museum.  They marveled at the magnificent specimens they could never dream of finding.  That would be a satisfying vacation.

When Gemma did that trip, she picked up a junonia’s tail end.  She found it digging through an ignored sandbank higher up the beach.  Later, she walked through the water at Lover’s Key and Fort Myers.  Boated out to inaccessible islands.  Even Marco Island was a hit despite the forums claiming nothing was there.

She left with a bucket of banded and true tulips.  Horse, fighting, and crown conchs.  A 9-inch lighting whelk.  And then there were the angel wings.  Spiny and leafy jewel boxes.  Apple, lace, and rose murexes.  Florida and alphabet cones.  Egg cockles.  Too many venuses to count.  And a rainbow of scallops and coquinas.

It took four serving trays to lay it out.  A fish-shaped bowl in her kitchen overflowed with the kitten paw clams from that trip.

One day, she would visit that area again.  When she was old.  When the hardcore trips became too difficult for her.  Now, in her youth, there were new places to scout.

She had already wandered along Florida’s panhandle.  Snorkeled along and between the Middle Keys.  Hit southern Georgia’s islands.  Tried the Gulf’s wild west on Mustang Island.  She even used her broken Spanish exploring Mexico’s islands.

This year would be different.  Her collection was maturing.  It included specimen-grade examples of most Gulf shells and many Atlantic ones.  She was saving for a trip to the Philippines to start fresh.  That would take a few years yet.  This trip was focused on a specific variant: a black olive.

Olive shells were her favorite.  Glossy with a wide color and size range.  The crowning 3-inch lettered olives.  The white variant netted olives.  The ubiquitous rice olives, barely visible among the sand.  She loved them all.  However, Florida was not supposed to have black olives.

A recent Reddit post claimed to have found one and provided a photo.  The shell in question was not the Philippines’ black olive that Gemma had her eyes on for the future.  The lettered olive in the picture was so dark that its characters were near invisible.

The thread devolved into arguing whether the specimen was Photoshop magic.  Gemma had the same question herself, but she liked to dream.  To imagine she could find it.  And the original poster had been bold enough to claim a location: Little Talbot Island.

Gemma had never explored the Jacksonville area.  Plus, it had beaches known for shark teeth.  She could harvest them if the black olive hunt was a wild goose chase.  Even if the teeth somehow fell through, she would have a good time.

Being out there meant more than coming home with a trunkful of snail bones.  Drifting through waves under sunshine.  Creeping through shaded mangroves.  Climbing over rock jetties.  Squishing through stinky bays of sea grass and hermit crabs.  Communing with nature.  That was the experience.

Even the trip to Florida was part of that.  Gemma rolled her windows down to smell the land changing from forest to farm to coast.  Salt-splashed breeze cooled her as she detoured onto scenic route A1A.  It took her through Amelia Island, Big and Little Talbot Island, and a half dozen nameless fishermen’s pull-offs.  She would check out the pull-offs if her planned beaches were a bust.

By the time she reached the rental house in Jax Beaches, it was evening.  She lugged her tools inside.  Made a sandwich.  Tossed the remaining food into the fridge.  Collapsing onto the couch, she turned on the news.

After the local channel caught her up on town gossip and recent murders, she flipped to the weather channel.  All she wanted was tomorrow’s forecast.  Instead, the anchors twittered about tomorrow’s king tide and potential flooding.  It would be the strongest one that year.

Gemma yawned through a mouthful of Colby-Jack and salami.  She already knew about the king tide.  About the bounty its peak would bring and its trough reveal.  It was why she chose this week.

That and because the waters would be cold enough to keep anyone not serious about diving out of the water.  Friendly competition suited her fine.  Dodging around screaming children and lethargic, creaky elders cramped her style.

Finally, the program shifted to the forecast.  Clear morning.  Spotty rain in the afternoon.  Perfect.  Low tide started at a comfortable 7:30 am.  Ideally, it would have been more like 9:30 am, since the state park opened at 8:00 am, but what could she do?  Society rarely operated on a schedule that respected nature’s rhythm.

The TV flicked off.  Gemma stretched widely, the hoop bracelets around her wrists jangling.  She had forgotten she was wearing them.  She pulled them and her earrings off on her way to the bedroom.   

Stowing them carefully in a decorative wood box, she clamped its lock.  Jewelry had no place in her life this week.  Her body would be adorned with swimming gear and sunscreen.  Nothing else.  She set her alarm for 6:00 am and turned in early.

---

When the alarm went off, Gemma was already breakfasting on the last cookies.  Excitement had made sleep difficult.  There would be no such problem tonight.  Four-to-six hours of walking, climbing, swimming, and digging was always enough to knock her out.  If anyone was desperate to cure insomnia, this was how.

She changed out of her pajamas.  For today’s outing, she chose her one-piece swimsuit.  The white one with pink, orange, and yellow hibiscus flowers.

After slathering on suntan lotion, she pulled on a silky, long-sleeved rash shirt.  Her oversized, green sunglasses came next.  Just in case.  The snorkeling equipment was already by the door with the mesh diving bags.

Ready to go, but with time to kill, she skimmed through her shell books.  Mostly, the photo plates.  She wondered if they would help this trip.  It seemed unlikely she would find anything new.

Reaching the olive photos, her smile kinked right.  She pulled up the Reddit thread on her phone.  Her glance alternated between it and the book.  Willing black olives into existence.  Populating a colony into a secret shallow off Little Talbot’s coast.

Her diving watch went off.  7:20 am.  Time to make history.  She snagged two chilled water bottles on her way to the car.  A quick jaunt into town got her from 295 onto Heckscher.  Hitting A1A put her back into the beach grove.

The sun rose over the ocean between the trees and houses.  A pelican troop flew over a bridge.  Windows down.  The wind tangling her hair.  She shivered in the morning breeze.  This was the closest Florida ever came to winter.

And it was beautifully quiet.  Only the occasional seagull whine or rasping grackle broke the silence.  If heaven was anything but this, Gemma and god would have a chat when she got there.

The Corolla rolled up to Little Talbot’s guard station at 8:01 am.  After paying the entry fee, Gemma rumbled past the first parking lot.  She had read that the best spot for shells was the island’s south end.  The plan was to start there and head straight into the ocean.

Gemma parked when the road ended.  Kicking off her driving shoes, she stepped into her sandals.  She dropped her gear and a water bottle into the bucket with her towel and dive flag.  Heaving it up, she carried it over the long boardwalk through the dunes.

Despite her excitement, she took her time crossing.  Few beaches had hilly dunes.  The ones in Texas were brimming with long grass, cactus, and, if you were lucky, tiny deer.

These had weedy flowers.  Pampas grass tufts.  Scrubby bushes.  Occasional short trees and squat palms.  Road signs had warned about gopher tortoises.  She hoped to meet one before the trip was over.

At the boardwalk’s end, a staircase eased down a 3-foot sand cliff to the beach.  Gemma hesitated there.  The steep drop reminded her of finding the junonia piece on Sanibel.  But today was not for digging.  Today was for exploring unknown waters.

Placing the bucket by the stairs, she dropped her sandals into it and pulled her mask and fins out.  She pre-emptively downed half the water bottle.  The rest was for when she got back.  As she replaced the bottle, something half-buried in the sand caught her eye.  A flashed smile.

She pulled a gorgeous, tan lettered olive from the sand.  A good sign.  It fell into the bucket.  Holding that moment, she breathed salt air in deep.  The heat permeated her skin.  The sand crumbled between her toes.  She was just missing one thing.  She sprinted to the sea.

Tumbling into the chilly water, a shocked laugh escaped her.  It was infinitely warmer than the frozen lakes up north.  Still, she would have to be careful.  Staying out too long would make the trip back difficult.  Maybe even dangerous.  It was about 8:30 am by then.  She set her watch for two hours.

Once her swim was over, she planned to towel off and walk the beach.  A good way to warm up and build an appetite for that enticing chowder place she saw on the way.  After lunch, her day would be spent picking barnacles and worm trails off her finds.  Bleaching away algae.  Oiling anything worthy of that extra shine.

Donning her mask and fins, Gemma glided through the shallows.  The water was clear.  Tiny yellow and black zebra fish flitted around her.  The endless sandy bottom worried her though.

Her imagined black olive colony needed food.  Worms, small crustaceans, other snails.  These would congregate around grass beds or coral reef.  This endless aquatic desert was a death sentence for live shells.

She kicked her fins harder and wider.  Hoping to find anything besides more sand.  The water deepened to 15 feet.  Grew colder.  Gemma slowed down.  This was a very bad sign.

She scanned the left.  Nothing.  Ahead.  Nothing.  Right.  It shallowed.  Interesting.  She continued that way.

A grin as a green patch caught her eye.  Sea grass.  There was more up ahead.  She stopped when a sandy lump caught her attention.  She abandoned her dive flag to retrieve that mass.

Back at the surface, she flailed at the Styrofoam buoy.  Corralling it into her arm, she examined her prize.  A six-inch true tulip clutching a clam in its black foot.  Being out of the water startled it.  It released its prey and retracted into its shell.  Its brown operculum door closed on its admirer.

Technically, it was illegal to take live specimens from the state park.  Even with her fresh and saltwater fishing license.  Only… how far did the state park’s boundaries extend into the ocean?

Gemma squinted back at the distant beach.  She was probably too close.  Oh well.  The specimen was more white than patchy brown anyway.  She had better.  Finding it was the exciting part.

As a large mollusk predator, the tulip spoke volumes about the ecosystem.  About the presence of prey.  Leaving her dive flag again, Gemma placed the snail on the sea floor over the dropped clam.  Best to let nature pick up where it had left off.

From here, the grass bed extended east into the Atlantic.  Southward, it gave way to sandy bottom and the water shallowed.  A sandbar.  Maybe she could plant the dive flag there.  Continuing south, she searched for a suitable anchor.

A school of foot-and-a-half-long redfish fell in line with her.  Now that would be a lunch.  A cup of Manhattan clam chowder with a plate of redfish and fries.  She reached out to touch a fish tail.  The whole school darted into deeper waters.

No rocks revealed themselves, but a wooden beam jutted out of the sand.  Gemma tried to wrench it loose.  It held firm.  Nodding, she tied the dive flag’s white nylon cord to it.

The sandbar was even shallower ahead.  At its peak, she could sit on the bottom and have her shoulders above water.  A good spot to rest her legs when her time was up.  She expected the return trip to be low effort with the tide coming in.  But, if she hit a rip current, she would need her full strength to escape.

Enough planning.  There were snails to find.

With mermaid’s grace, she pushed back into the depths.  Hovering over the grass, she sought lumps, trails, and color.  Below her, an angel shark scouted for its own prey.  She had never seen one in real life before.  For a moment, she was tempted to dive down and touch its brown, fleshy fins.

She knew better.  If she wanted to pet sharks, she could go to an aquarium.  Harassing a wild one was asking to get bitten.  She moved along.

Soon after, she stumbled onto a second angel burying itself in the sand.  Then a third.  Clearly, this was a productive habitat to host so many large fish.

Her own quarry proved more elusive.  There were banded tulips and augers galore.  She even swam over a brilliant orange horse conch.  An immature specimen at four inches, but in a few decades, it would be this food chain’s apex predator.

The water shallowed again, patching between sand and sea grass.  A trail caught Gemma’s eye.  She dove for it and pulled out a dark, one-inch olive.  Not her black olive, but it was alive.  She held her hands underwater, waiting for the animal to creep out.

White with brown lettering, the foot cautiously extended.  Its tentacles and eyes popped out.  An oversized siphon tube emerged between its eyes, completing the sea unicorn.  She tapped the siphon, and the animal crumpled inward.  Returning it to the sand, she scanned for more.  Rows of siphons greeted her.

Dive after dive, she brushed sand aside to reveal each shell’s color.  Tan.  Mottled grey-brown.  Cream.  Mocha.  The color palettes repeated.

Finally, she stumbled onto a new one.  It was small but the darkest shade thus far.  She brought it to the surface for closer inspection.

The animal stood out.  Its muscled body was dark grey, unlike its creamy-fleshed neighbors.  A hint she was on the right track.  However, the shell was nothing like the Reddit photo.  In this case, it was more remarkable with its animal left alive.  She checked her watch and grimaced.  Thirty minutes left.

If only she had a saltwater tank.  And a way to transport this wonderful creature home without killing it.  After a deep breath, Gemma returned the olive to the sand.  Her search continued.

The ocean floor rose and fell as she swam further.  Like a butterfly sampling flowers, she visited each high point.  Her fingers sifted through loose sand for snails.  She found two more grey-bodied animals, but neither was as dark as the first.  Drifting over another trough, she almost missed the struggle below.

A bronze true tulip wrestled with another small mollusk.  What a beautiful color.  From the surface, it looked huge, but water refraction plays tricks on one’s eyes.  There was only one way to know for sure how big it was.

Gemma dove for it.  Pulled it up with both hands.  The shell was large and beautiful, yes, but also deeply flawed.  A healed crack marred its bell.  A scar.  That damage spoke to a vicious attack.  If the shell were empty, she would have taken it in a heartbeat.  As it was, the snail was a survivor.  It had earned the right to live out its days.

The snail was unaware of Gemma’s reverence.  It snapped into its shell, dropping its prey.  A shiny black flash sank into the ocean.  Gemma threw the tulip aside, shooting into the water after the escapee.  Her greedy hands swiped twice to snatch it.  An instant to resurface.

Her breathing tube popped out of her mouth as she gasped.  A trembling whisper, “Yes!  Yes!”  The olive was glossy jet black.  Its animal was charcoal grey with black lettering.  Its only color was a hint of purple under the shell’s lip.

It made sense.  Of course, it lived in the trough.  The shell was too dark for the shallows.  It would be an easy target there.  The deeper water hid it better.  How many more were down there?

She quick-scanned the depths.  Took a breath to dive again, but caught herself.  Her attention refocused on the specimen in hand.  Two inches.  Impressive.  But if she could get a full-sized one—

Her watch beeped.
	
“Oh, come on, not now!”  Ten more minutes would be fine.  Even fifteen!  After all, she was a strong swimmer.  The water was not <i>that</i> cold.

Practicality spoke over her mad rationalizations in a tone far too much like her mother’s:  <i>You have a whole week here.  Don’t act stupid now.</i>  Sighing, Gemma dropped the olive into her mesh bag.  Tomorrow, she would find it some friends.

For now, it was time to swim back to the dive flag, take a break, and then return to… Where was the dive flag?

She looked to the left and right.  Nothing.  The shoreline was out of view too.  But she knew where it had to be.  Or she thought she knew anyway.

She turned the rest of the way around.  Oh.  The flag was over <i>there?</i>  She tilted her head in confusion.  The buoy was closer than she thought it should have been.  Something was wrong.  

Holding position against the bobbing waves, Gemma tracked the flag’s path.  It drifted away.  A current had it.  It must have come untied from the beam.  Shore was not that way.

Breathing slowly to center herself, Gemma cautiously backtracked.  Once she found a disturbed olive bed, her pulse eased.  Those disruptions were proof that she was heading the right way.  Still, she picked up her pace.

Losing the flag made her vulnerable.  If a boat motored by, the sailors would not know to watch for her.  Unwanted mental images of scarred manatee backs from such collisions made her cringe.  At least they had blubber to protect themselves.  If she got into a fight with an engine, there would be nothing left of her.

A redfish school rocketed past, scaring her.  The areas she once considered shallows were now uncomfortably deep.  Bigger animals could navigate these depths.  It was unsafe to be this far out alone.  

More redfish blitzed through her peripheral vision.  An angel shark glided behind them.  A good sign.  The angels had been near the sandbar.

Gemma pushed herself harder.  Swam faster.  Told herself it was determination driving her.  Not fear.  

The water lightened ahead.  The sand inclined then plateaued.  But where was the sandbar?  She spotted the beam beneath her.  <i>This</i> was the sandbar.  She had underestimated how quickly the tide was rising.

Coiling white caught her eye.  Her nylon cord was still tied to the beam.  Confused, she moved closer.  Caught the frayed end.  It had been roughly cut by something jagged.  What could have done that?

She swam to the sandbar's highest point.  When she stood, the water lapped below her breasts.  Pulling off her mask, she scanned the horizon.  Shore was in view again, which helped.  It was a long swim away, though.  It had not seemed so far before.

The first hundred feet would be deep.  After that, she would be in her comfort zone.  To finish the first stretch quickly, she committed to resting now.  Accepted that the water would be deeper when she took it on.  A few more inches made little difference at this point.

Closing her eyes, Gemma concentrated on her breathing.  Focused on her prowess as a swimmer.  As a diver.  She had braved the open ocean in groups.  This would be the same.  Her eyes opened, glancing at her mesh bag.  It would all be worth it once she reached dry land.

A brown shadow sailed by.  An angel shark.  It cruised toward a disturbance brewing across the sandbar.  Redfish huddled close together.  Their fins nervously broke the water’s surface.  The bottom individuals fought for higher positions, forming a writhing ball.  Another angel swept in under them, scaring the fish into a tighter mass.

Gemma shook her head.  Angels occasionally scavenged, but they were ambush predators.  It was why they were so flat.  A lone individual would bury itself, wait for unsuspecting prey, and SMACK.  Dinner time.

Seeing three angels circling fish far too large to catch was bizarre.  And a fourth was joining the pack.  Soon there were six, corralling redfish like sheepdogs gathering the flock.

Suddenly, all six sharks sank to the sandbar’s bottom.  A moment of reprieve for the fish ball.  A moment for the component bodies to ease and slow.  A ten-foot bull shark torpedoed through the school.  A battering ram, snapping them in half.

Redfish bolted to either side.  One clump smacked into Gemma mid-scream, throwing her off balance.  Her arms cartwheeled.  She fought to stay upright.  Stumbling back, she managed it.  Looked up.

The angels rolled over each other.  Over a redfish tail hunk.  One victoriously swallowed the meal.  The others poked at the sand, seeking scraps.  One by one, they drifted into deeper water.
	
“They…”  Gemma glanced around.  She had no idea where the bull had gone.  She had missed its exit path.  No telltale fin broke the surface.  It could be anywhere.  Bulls were aggressive.  Territorial.  Had a reputation for attacking swimmers.  And this one was hungry.  Hunting.  “This isn’t good.  This is NOT good.”

Something rough brushed into Gemma’s leg.  She gave a blood-curdling scream.  The angel darted back into the depths.  Panicking was the worst thing she could do right now.  Thrashing in the water would attract the bull.  She clenched her fists and forced her frantic breathing to slow.  She was dizzy from panting so fast.

The angel swept by again.  And again.  It circled Gemma.

She hissed, “I.  Am not.  A fish.”

As if to contradict her, another angel entered the circle.

“I’m not a fish!  Do you see these!”  She splayed her hands over the water.  “Fingers!  Fish do not have fingers!”  A third angel joined.

Gemma took a deep breath.  Put her mask back on.  Shifted into floating position.  The circle around her widened into an oval.  As she kicked forward, the last three angels sped in to fill gaps.

At first, the shark halo drifted with her.  However, it stopped advancing when she reached the sandbar’s edge.  Almost running into an angel, she startled back.  She tried to dive under them, but they went down with her.  Their sharp skin abraded her arms.

They had her trapped.  <i>Intentionally.</i>  She knew sharks were intelligent, but not like this.

Standing again, Gemma looked for the bull.  No fin.  No terrified redfish.  No massive, stout body ominously stalking the depths.  The angels broke into two stacked circles around her, leaving no space to resume swimming position.

She needed a weapon.  Checked her gear.  The mask.  The breathing tube.  The mesh bag.  She brandished the plastic breathing tube.  At least it would put some distance between her hands and the angels.  She raised it over her head.  Watched the circles go around one last time.  Bashed an angel over the head.
	
The sharks frenzied.  Teeth gnashing wildly.  Ramming into Gemma.  Into one another.  Her breathing tube bounced off one shark after another.  A bite landed on her arm.  Another on her opposite leg.  She curled into herself, hugging her damaged limbs.  The angels swept away from her and off the sandbar.  Stringy red swirled in the water around her.

Gemma’s injuries were minor, but the bull would smell the blood.  Gritting her teeth, she grounded herself against the pain.  Steeled her will to move forward.

An angel resumed circling around her.  Another joined it.  They stayed well out of reach.

Her will faltered.  She checked her watch.  Hours until high tide.  The next low was early evening.  The sharks would be more active then.  Assuming they did not come for her during the day.  Assuming she could manage the cold for that long.

There was no option.  She had to swim now.  With the angels accompanying her like vultures.  With the hungry bull lurking unseen.
