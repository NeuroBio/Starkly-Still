A squat spider crawled from the brush into the late afternoon sunlight illuminating the back patio.  It preferred these transitional spaces, where the natural world gave way to concrete and steel.  Its many eyes processed the terrain.

The bushes lining the house were already crowded with webs.  The long, featureless stone slabs were a brutal desert.  But the bistro table by the house was an interesting vantage point.  Worth a closer look.

Eight legs skittered with precision.  Across the emptiness.  Up a textured metal chair leg.  Over the plastic cushion’s weave.  From the chair’s back, the spider saw its new home.

The diagonal distance between where it stood and the screen door was perfect.  From there, a web’s body would hover a foot over the bushes.  Across the patio, gnats swarmed at that height.  Once they drifted this way, the spider would have food for weeks.  No time to lose.

Dabbing silk on the chair, the spider rappelled to the concrete.  It rushed to the door.  Scaled the screen.  Found the right place to plant the first connector.  It chewed excess silk, shortening the thread into a taut beam.  Then it watched.  Would the beam hold?

The silk listed in the breeze but held firm.

Satisfied, the spider laid another silk bead and ran across the beam.  It took three sweeps to make a sturdy bridge.  On the final, testing tug, the silk resisted just enough.

The fourth pass required a new type of thread.  A long one.  As the spider darted over the bridge, the thread dangled into an arc.  Once tacked to the screen, it shaped the web into a wide smile.

The spider scurried back to the middle.  Descending on a new thread from the bridge center, it reached the mouth’s bottom lip.  It knotted those two strands and continued down another inch.  It stayed there, swaying like a trapeze artist posing for the crowd.

In truth, it was struggling with a problem.  The web should be horizontal, not vertical.  Its eyes swiveled.  The house’s corner was too far.  The bushes could be used, but that departed from the original vision.

The other chair back might do.  Except it was farther away than would be desired.  But the alternative was a journey over the concrete, to the mulch, through the bushes, and up the wall to find another spot.  All while trying to keep this thread intact.  That settled it.

Landing, the spider zipped to the other chair.  It placed the anchor thread and tightened it.  The web’s frame sharpened into a crisp triangle.  So far, so good.

The next steps were trivial.  Three more threads crossed the frame, creating a hexagon—the web’s true boundary.  Two dozen axial threads sliced the hexagon into wedges.  On top of them, a quick series of concentric circles.  Guidelines to ensure the next threads would be correctly positioned.

Resting in a corner, the spider brooded over its next move.  The work thus far would be meaningless unless the web could ensnare prey.  The trap filaments required precise alignment.  The question was, would they be more accurate working inwards or outwards?

<i>Begin the spiral.</i>

Instinct took over.  The spider scrambled to the web center and laid the first sticky threads.  Its pace was swift.  Its movements methodical.  A muscle memory inherited at birth.

As dusk fell, the final trap threads settled.  Ready for the gnats come morning.  Or, if the spider was lucky, a moth overnight.  What a feast a moth would be.

Even weary, the spider remained agile and sleek.  It danced to the web center to rest.  Technically, there was still the matter of constructing a hidey hole to attend to.  But that could be done at the spider’s leisure later that night.  Now, amid dying sunbeams, was the time to inspect its golden, gleaming masterpiece.

One of the axial threads was slightly off.

The spider's gaze fixed on it.  A clawed leg reached out to tug the thread.  Pulled it in line.  It reverted to its aberrant position.  The spider tugged again, harder.  The thread had no remaining give.

Grooming its jaws nervously, the spider rotated left, then right.  Everything else was perfect.  How could this one strand have gone so wrong?  And with no way to fix it… was it truly necessary?

The spider snapped it.  Chasing the problem through the web, it chewed off segment after segment.  Sticky threads depending on it sagged.  They were devoured next.  New ones were placed.  The spider returned to the web center.

The web had shifted.

With the offending thread gone, its once equal tension became unbalanced.  The spiral deviated from its true center.  The spider snapped a second thread to rebalance the strain.  The web kinked in another direction.  Breaking the third, the distortion spread.

The spider grappled with threads until it lost track of what it was doing.  Lost the destruction’s purpose.  A new instinct kicked in.

<i>Consume the old web.</i>

It was irresistible.  Every silk line was cut, chewed, and swallowed.  There was no pattern.  No order.  Just simple and absolute demolition.

The web collapsed inwards, becoming a wavy tangle spinning from the bridge thread.  A stray breeze blew sticky threads onto the spider’s back.  It needed to stop.  To re-evaluate.  To disentangle itself.

Instead, it kept eating.  More silk wrapped around its body, its legs, its eyes.

Devolved into a white ball, the spider finally came to its senses.  Only three legs twitched when it commanded all eight to move.  It awkwardly ascended the last axial thread.

At the apex, it clung to the bridge.  The walk to the chair back was long.  But if it could reach that safe, solid surface, it could eat away its casing.  There was no option but to try.

Tentatively, the spider pulled itself forward.  It did not think to check its dark surroundings.  It did not sense the insect approaching.  It had no idea what happened when a moth smashed into the bridge.  Fluttering madly, the moth bounced off and flew towards a lamp.

The spider was not so lucky.  It had spun around the bridge during the collision.  Shooting silk in all directions had ensured it held on.  But it had mistakenly produced more sticky threads.  The spider could no longer move forward.  Its cocoon was complete.
