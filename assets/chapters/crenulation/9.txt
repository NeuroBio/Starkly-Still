A damp night in a forgotten alley in some bustling city’s outskirts.  Disgruntled drivers in need of a drink honk at those who had imbibed too much already.  A mourning dove perched on a windowsill coos gently.  Steaming haze erupts from a jarred manhole.  Flickering blue and red neon signs illuminate the alley like cop lights.

The journal of collected stories lies on an overturned, metal trashcan lid.  Its pages ruffle in the chilly air as if wheezing.  Something is terribly wrong.

The journal’s cover bloats with rainwater and dew.  It should have been impervious to this.  It always had been.  Whether basking in muggy rainforest or drifting along ocean depths, its sheer will provided a shield.  As long as it knew what was coming, it could repel anything.  Until tonight.  Tonight, it is vulnerable to everything.

A new location.  A safe place.  The journal has to <i>move</i>.

No clear haven comes to it.  Its foggy sentience mirrors the alley’s thick haze.  Repairs must be attempted here.  There is no other option.

In sharp, jerking motions, its binding threads unwind.  The journal regards its pages one-by-one.  Damp, but fine.  Damp, but fine.  Damp, but—

Impatiently, the journal flips through itself.  It is in pain.  The full appraisal can wait.  Something recent brought cancer into the volume.  Surgical excision of the source is paramount.  The only path to relief.

A third of the way through, a page feels wrong.  The journal flips back to it.  Crunchy, crumbly dust scrapes against the book’s binding as the sick page turns again.  The journal freezes as it beholds the creeping illness on beloved page 36.

A swirling, coppery brown residue seeps from the ink.  The stiff page’s edges are tattered, chipped, and ragged.  Its wood pulp surface flakes like dead skin after a sunburn.  Its words are illegible.  The journal waited too long to address this.

It had sensed something off a week prior.  A flighty, unfocused anxiety.  But that was nothing new.  The journal had previously experienced dis-ease while empathizing with its pages.  With their characters.  Feeling a story’s emotions frightened it.

Such fears came and went.  In hindsight, the journal regarded emotional contagion as a trophy.  Proof that its collection had reached a new high.  That the words’ power was now great enough to distort the world around them.  Worrying about it was foolish.

It had been wrong to ignore this feeling.

The afflicted story is unsalvageable.  Even if the infection clears, the page will be honeycombed with holes.  Words annihilated.  What remained reads as jumbled, schizophrenic cries blindly groping for coherence.  The word “shark” still reads clearly.  Stories about sharks are a dime a dozen.  Not that the journal remembers any of them.  Regardless, it knows page 36 is replaceable.

The page ignites.

Flame cleanses the rot. The excess moisture beads up as it leaves the paper and leather.   The journal breathes freely.  A new story will soon replace page 36.  The journal’s only regret is that it cannot remember the incinerated entry’s contents.  Avoiding stories with similar ailments will prove impossible.
